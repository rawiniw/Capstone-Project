<FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">April 27, 2018</FONT></P>
<P style="text-align: left;">
<FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">We urge you to j</FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">oin us in voting FOR Item 4 &#147;</FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">Shareholder Proposal Regarding Diverse Board Candidates,</FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">&#148; at Amazon.com, Inc.&#146;s 2018 Annual Meeting of Shareholders </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">on May 30, 2018. This </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">proposal calls on the Amazon board of directors to implement a &#147;Rooney Rule&#148; </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">requiring that the initial list of candidates from which new management-supported director nominees are chosen should include (but need not be limited to) qualified women and minority candidates. The Rooney Rule originated in the National Football League, where teams are required to interview at least one minority candidate for any open head coaching or general manager position. In the corporate sector, the Rooney Rule has been successfully implemented in the director search process by many companies, including Microsoft and Costco.</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">i</FONT></SUP></P>
<P style="text-align: left;">
<FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">Shareholders have long believed that embracing diversity will benefit companies by providing greater access to talent, harnessing existing talent more effectively, and improving decision making by reducing groupthink and similar psychological biases. There is now ample evidence in support of this belief: in a 2017 study of over 1,000 corporations in 12 countries, McKinsey found that companies with the most diverse leadership (top quartile) for gender were 21% more likely to earn above average profits, while those in the top quartile for ethnic/cultural diversity were 33% more likely to outperform. Moreover, McKinsey found that companies in the bottom tier of leadership diversity (assessed on both gender and ethnic/cultural) were 29% less likely to achieve above average profitability than all companies studied.</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">ii </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">Research from a wide variety of sources, including the law firm Paul Hastings, the Peterson Institute for International Economics, and Credit Suisse, further bolsters the shareholder case for diversity.</FONT></P>
<P style="text-align: left;">
<FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">While Amazon&#146;s board articulates support for diversity among directors and executives, it has failed to </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">translate that verbal support into practical action. In particular, the Amazon board does not include any racial or ethnic minority directors. This stands in contrast to the 17 retail, Internet, technology, and media companies Amazon identifies as peers in its current proxy statement, each of which has at least one director of color, while 10 of the 17 have at least three directors of color. Additionally, only six of</FONT></P>
<P style="text-align: left;">
<FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">Amazon&#146;s top 105 executives are Asian, only one is Hispanic or Latino, and none are Black or African </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">American.</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">iii </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">The underrepresentation of people of color in Amazon&#146;s leadership is at odds with its U.S. </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">workforce, which is fairly diverse (21% Black, 13% Asian, 13% Hispanic),</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">iv </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">and may limit the board and </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">the executive team&#146;s ability to identify business opportunitie</FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">s and risks: for instance, in 2016 Bloomberg found that in six major same-</FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">day delivery cities, Amazon&#146;s service area excluded predominantly Black zip </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">codes (Amazon has subsequently expanded same-day delivery in at least three of these cities),</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">v </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">while a rep</FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">ort by Color of Change found that Amazon&#146;s streaming service was one of eight networks at which </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">more than 90% of shows had zero or one Black writer, and that only one of 16 Amazon shows examined had a non-white showrunner.</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">vi</FONT></SUP></P>
<P style="text-align: left;">
<FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">Though the Amazon board includes three female directors, among its 17 self-identified peers, a majority (nine of 17) have four or more female directors. Moreover, </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">Amazon&#146;s ongoing diversity and inclusion </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">issues suggest the company could use more women in leadership. According to the </FONT><I><FONT size=2 face="Calibri-Italic,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">New York Times</FONT></I><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">, only one of the 16 executives reporting directly to the CEO is a woman,</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">vii </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">while </FONT><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">Amazon&#146;s EEOC filings indicate that only 23 of Amazon&#146;s top 105 executives are women (22%).</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">viii </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face="Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif">Again, Amazon&#146;s leadership is</FONT></P>

<P style="TEXT-ALIGN: left"><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>less diverse than its workforce: women comprise 39% of Amazon employees globally.</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>ix </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>The website Fairygoodboss.com, which allows women to rate their employers in terms of fairness and opportunity, has ranked Amazon far behind its major technology peers in each of the last two years.</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>x </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>Finally, the Roy Price sexual harassment scandal </FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>&#8211; in which Amazon was aware of Mr. Price&#8217;s harassing behavior for </FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>nearly two years before taking action to remove him</FONT><SUP><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>xi </FONT></SUP><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>&#8211; </FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>evinces a further basis for concern that the company has not established a tone at the top which regards harassment as unacceptable and diversity as a critical goal.</FONT></P>

<P style="TEXT-ALIGN: left"><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>With six director departures in the last 10 years, Amazon has had numerous opportunities to add gender, racial, and ethnic diversity to its board. In addition, its corporate governance guidelines have </FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>stated that &#8220;the Nominating and Corporate Governance Committee&#8230; seeks out candidates with a </FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>diversity of experience and perspectives</FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>&#8221; during that entire period. </FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>But the low-diversity status quo has persisted. </FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>Amazon&#8217;s two most recent direc</FONT><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>tor appointments have been white men, both initially recommended by existing Amazon directors. In fact, the last time Amazon disclosed that a new director was not initially recommended by an existing director was 2010 (with the exception that Amazon provided no disclosure for an appointment in 2014). Board diversity at Amazon would be promoted by more meaningful external searches that also include women and minorities as candidates. For example, at Microsoft and Costco, the Rooney Rule has contributed to diverse director appointments: after adopting new policies, Microsoft has appointed three men and three women, one of whom is a person of color, and Costco has appointed one woman and two men, one of whom is a person of color.</FONT></P>

<P style="TEXT-ALIGN: left"><FONT size=2 face=Calibri,Arial,Helvetica,sans-serif>Our Rooney Rule proposal establishes a modest but demonstrably effective requirement that we believe will accelerate the transition from talk to action at Amazon. Please join us in voting FOR Item 4 "Shareholder Proposal Regarding Diverse Candidates."</FONT></P>
