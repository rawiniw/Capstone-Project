interested in the question, how do you fend off Day 2? What are the techniques and tactics? How do you keep the vitality of Day 1, even inside a large organization? </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT
STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Such a question can&#146;t have a simple answer. There will be many elements, multiple paths, and many traps. I don&#146;t know the whole answer, but I may know bits of it. Here&#146;s a starter pack of
essentials for Day 1 defense: customer obsession, a skeptical view of proxies, the eager adoption of external trends, and high-velocity decision making. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:18px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT
STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">True Customer Obsession </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">There are many ways to center a business. You can be competitor focused,
you can be product focused, you can be technology focused, you can be business model focused, and there are more. But in my view, obsessive customer focus is by far the most protective of Day 1 vitality. </FONT></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Why? There are many advantages to a customer-centric approach, but here&#146;s the big one: customers are <I>always</I> beautifully, wonderfully
dissatisfied, even when they report being happy and business is great. Even when they don&#146;t yet know it, customers want something better, and your desire to delight customers will drive you to invent on their behalf. No customer ever asked
Amazon to create the Prime membership program, but it sure turns out they wanted it, and I could give you many such examples. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Staying in Day
1 requires you to experiment patiently, accept failures, plant seeds, protect saplings, and double down when you see customer delight. A customer-obsessed culture best creates the conditions where all of that can happen. </FONT></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:18px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Resist Proxies </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">As companies get larger and
more complex, there&#146;s a tendency to manage to proxies. This comes in many shapes and sizes, and it&#146;s dangerous, subtle, and very Day 2. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT
STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">A common example is process as proxy. Good process serves you so you can serve customers. But if you&#146;re not watchful, the process can become the thing. This can happen very easily in large
organizations. The process becomes the proxy for the result you want. You stop looking at outcomes and just make sure you&#146;re doing the process right. Gulp. It&#146;s not that rare to hear a junior leader defend a bad outcome with something
like, &#147;Well, we followed the process.&#148; A more experienced leader will use it as an opportunity to investigate and improve the process. The process is not the thing. It&#146;s always worth asking, do we own the process or does the process
own us? In a Day 2 company, you might find it&#146;s the second. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Another example: market research and customer surveys can become proxies for
customers &#150; something that&#146;s especially dangerous when you&#146;re inventing and designing products. &#147;Fifty-five percent of beta testers report being satisfied with this feature. That is up from 47% in the first survey.&#148;
That&#146;s hard to interpret and could unintentionally mislead. </FONT></P>

<p Style='page-break-before:always'>
<HR  SIZE="3" style="COLOR:#999999" WIDTH="100%" ALIGN="CENTER">

 <P STYLE="margin-top:0px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Good inventors and designers <I>deeply</I> understand their customer. They spend tremendous energy
developing that intuition. They study and understand many anecdotes rather than only the averages you&#146;ll find on surveys. They <I>live</I> with the design. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT
STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">I&#146;m not against beta testing or surveys. But you, the product or service owner, must understand the customer, have a vision, and love the offering. Then, beta testing and research can help you find
your blind spots. A remarkable customer experience starts with heart, intuition, curiosity, play, guts, taste. You won&#146;t find any of it in a survey. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:18px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT
STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Embrace External Trends </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">The outside world can push you into Day 2 if you won&#146;t or can&#146;t
embrace powerful trends quickly. If you fight them, you&#146;re probably fighting the future. Embrace them and you have a tailwind. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">These big
trends are not that hard to spot (they get talked and written about a lot), but they can be strangely hard for large organizations to embrace. We&#146;re in the middle of an obvious one right now: machine learning and artificial intelligence.
</FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Over the past decades computers have broadly automated tasks that programmers could describe with clear rules and algorithms. Modern machine
learning techniques now allow us to do the same for tasks where describing the precise rules is much harder. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">At Amazon, we&#146;ve been
engaged in the practical application of machine learning for many years now. Some of this work is highly visible: our autonomous Prime Air delivery drones; the Amazon Go convenience store that uses machine vision to eliminate checkout lines; and
Alexa,</FONT><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="1"><SUP STYLE="vertical-align:baseline; position:relative; bottom:.8ex">1</SUP></FONT><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2"> our cloud-based AI assistant. (We still struggle
to keep Echo in stock, despite our best efforts. A high-quality problem, but a problem. We&#146;re working on it.) </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">But much of what we do
with machine learning happens beneath the surface. Machine learning drives our algorithms for demand forecasting, product search ranking, product and deals recommendations, merchandising placements, fraud detection, translations, and much more.
Though less visible, much of the impact of machine learning will be of this type &#150; quietly but meaningfully improving core operations. </FONT></P>
<P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Inside AWS, we&#146;re excited to lower the costs and barriers to machine learning and AI so organizations of all sizes can take advantage of these
advanced techniques. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Using our pre-packaged versions of popular deep learning frameworks running on P2 compute instances (optimized for this
workload), customers are already developing powerful systems ranging everywhere from early disease detection to increasing crop yields. And we&#146;ve also made Amazon&#146;s higher level services available in a convenient form. Amazon Lex
(what&#146;s inside Alexa), Amazon Polly, and Amazon Rekognition remove the heavy lifting from natural language understanding, speech generation, and image analysis. They can be accessed with simple API calls &#150; no machine learning expertise
required. Watch this space. Much more to come. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:18px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">High-Velocity Decision Making </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:6px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT
STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Day 2 companies make high-<I>quality</I> decisions, but they make high-quality decisions <I>slowly</I>. To keep the energy and dynamism of Day 1, you have to somehow make high-quality,
<I>high-velocity</I> decisions. Easy for start-ups and very challenging for large organizations. The senior team at Amazon is determined to keep our decision-making velocity high. Speed matters in business &#150; plus a high-velocity decision making
environment is more fun too. We don&#146;t know all the answers, but here are some thoughts. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">First, never use a one-size-fits-all
decision-making process. Many decisions are reversible, two-way doors. Those decisions can use a light-weight process. For those, so what if you&#146;re wrong? I wrote about this in more detail in last year&#146;s letter. </FONT></P>
<P STYLE="line-height:8px;margin-top:0px;margin-bottom:2px;border-bottom:0.5pt solid #000000;width:10%">&nbsp;</P>

 <P STYLE="margin-top:0px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Second, most decisions should probably be made with somewhere around 70% of the information you wish you
had. If you wait for 90%, in most cases, you&#146;re probably being slow. Plus, either way, you need to be good at quickly recognizing and correcting bad decisions. If you&#146;re good at course correcting, being wrong may be less costly than you
think, whereas being slow is going to be expensive for sure. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Third, use the phrase &#147;disagree and commit.&#148; This phrase will save a
lot of time. If you have conviction on a particular direction even though there&#146;s no consensus, it&#146;s helpful to say, &#147;Look, I know we disagree on this but will you gamble with me on it? Disagree and commit?&#148; By the time
you&#146;re at this point, no one can know the answer for sure, and you&#146;ll probably get a quick yes. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">This isn&#146;t one way. If
you&#146;re the boss, you should do this too. I disagree and commit all the time. We recently greenlit a particular Amazon Studios original. I told the team my view: debatable whether it would be interesting enough, complicated to produce, the
business terms aren&#146;t that good, and we have lots of other opportunities. They had a completely different opinion and wanted to go ahead. I wrote back right away with &#147;I disagree and commit and hope it becomes the most watched thing
we&#146;ve ever made.&#148; Consider how much slower this decision cycle would have been if the team had actually had to <I>convince</I> me rather than simply get my commitment. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT
STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Note what this example is not: it&#146;s not me thinking to myself &#147;well, these guys are wrong and missing the point, but this isn&#146;t worth me chasing.&#148; It&#146;s a genuine disagreement of
opinion, a candid expression of my view, a chance for the team to weigh my view, and a quick, sincere commitment to go their way. And given that this team has already brought home 11 Emmys, 6 Golden Globes, and 3 Oscars, I&#146;m just glad they let
me in the room at all! </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">Fourth, recognize true <I>misalignment</I> issues early and escalate them <I>immediately</I>. Sometimes teams have
different objectives and fundamentally different views. They are not aligned. No amount of discussion, no number of meetings will resolve that deep misalignment. Without escalation, the default dispute resolution mechanism for this scenario is
exhaustion. Whoever has more stamina carries the decision. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">I&#146;ve seen many examples of sincere misalignment at Amazon over the years.
When we decided to invite third party sellers to compete directly against us on our own product detail pages &#150; that was a big one. Many smart, well-intentioned Amazonians were simply not at all aligned with the direction. The big decision set
up hundreds of smaller decisions, many of which needed to be escalated to the senior team. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">&#147;You&#146;ve worn me down&#148; is an awful
decision-making process. It&#146;s slow and de-energizing. Go for quick escalation instead &#150; it&#146;s better. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">So, have you settled only
for decision quality, or are you mindful of decision velocity too? Are the world&#146;s trends tailwinds for you? Are you falling prey to proxies, or do they serve you? And most important of all, are you delighting customers? We can have the scope
and capabilities of a large company and the spirit and heart of a small one. But we have to choose it. </FONT></P> <P STYLE="margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:0px"><FONT STYLE="font-family:Times New Roman" SIZE="2">A huge thank you to each and every
