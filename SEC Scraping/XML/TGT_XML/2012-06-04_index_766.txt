<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="FONT-VARIANT: small-caps; DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt; FONT-WEIGHT: bold">Corporate political spending is fraught with risk and increasingly unpopular with the general public</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt; FONT-WEIGHT: bold">Corporate political spending is risky</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt"><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; DISPLAY: inline">The Supreme Court&#8217;s January 2010 </font><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; FONT-STYLE: italic; DISPLAY: inline">Citizens United</font><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; DISPLAY: inline"> decision reversed the long standing prohibition on the unlimited use of corporate treasury funds for political purposes. Shareholders are increasingly concerned that such funds, which can be funneled to SuperPACS, trade associations and other organizations for political use, often without proper oversight or board and shareholder knowledge, could create a risk to shareholder value.&#160;</font></font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">In 2010, Target experienced firsthand the backlash and impact that making controversial contributions can have.</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt"><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; DISPLAY: inline">A </font><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; FONT-STYLE: italic; DISPLAY: inline">Bloomberg Business Week</font><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; DISPLAY: inline"> piece summed up the situation as follows:</font></font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 36pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 36pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt"><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; DISPLAY: inline">&#8220;Target (</font><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">TGT</font><font style="BACKGROUND-COLOR: #ffffff; DISPLAY: inline">), the second-largest U.S. discount retailer, has become an early test case of a Supreme Court ruling that allows companies to spend unlimited amounts to sway voters in federal races. It donated to a business advocacy group, which used the funds to run an ad supporting a socially conservative Republican candidate for governor of Minnesota. Along the way, Target enraged the gay community, offering up a cautionary tale for other corporations about taking sides in political campaigns.&#8221;</font><font style="FONT-SIZE: 70%; VERTICAL-ALIGN: text-top">1</font></font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">In that case, the company made a $150,000 contribution to MN Forward, a group supporting Tom Emmer, a gubernatorial candidate in Minnesota who opposed marriage equality. In response to the donation, the company experienced boycotts, public backlash and degradation of employee morale.<font style="FONT-SIZE: 70%; VERTICAL-ALIGN: text-top">2</font></font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">In another example, activists launched boycotts of Valero and Tesoro in 2010, after the two Texas-based oil companies contributed millions to a campaign to repeal California legislation designed to reduce greenhouse gas emissions.<font style="FONT-SIZE: 70%; VERTICAL-ALIGN: text-top">3</font></font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">Because it is extremely difficult to determine which contributions may be considered controversial, and very small monetary amounts can expose the company to significant backlash, the proponent contends that it is in Target&#8217;s best interest to eliminate the risk entirely by refraining from political campaign contributions.</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt; FONT-WEIGHT: bold">Corporate political campaign spending is becoming increasingly unpopular among the general public</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">In the two election cycles following the US Supreme Court&#8217;s landmark <font style="FONT-STYLE: italic; DISPLAY: inline">Citizens United </font>decision<font style="FONT-SIZE: 70%; VERTICAL-ALIGN: text-top">4</font>, an unprecedented amount of money has flowed into national and state-level political elections. Spending by outside groups between the 2006 and 2010 mid-term elections rose from nearly $69 million to approximately $305 million in those cycles.<font style="FONT-SIZE: 70%; VERTICAL-ALIGN: text-top">5</font> The number of registered Super PACs alone rose from a mere 17 in August 2010 to 264 by December 2011.</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">These developments are deeply unpopular among the U.S. public. In February 2010, an ABC News/Washington Post poll found that 80% opposed </font><font style="FONT-STYLE: italic; DISPLAY: inline; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">Citizens United</font><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">, noting, &#8220;[t]he bipartisan nature of these views is striking in these largely partisan times.&#8221;</font><font style="FONT-SIZE: 70%; VERTICAL-ALIGN: text-top">6</font><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-SIZE: 10pt"> In a June 2010 Harris poll, 90% of those polled said that big companies &#8220;have too much influence in Washington.&#8221;</font> <font style="FONT-SIZE: 70%; VERTICAL-ALIGN: text-top">7</font></font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">In this presidential primary season, there has been an explosion of media coverage devoted to the rise of SuperPacs, 501(c)(4) organizations and other vehicles for unlimited and often secret spending that have emerged from the loopholes affirmed by <font style="FONT-STYLE: italic; DISPLAY: inline">Citizens United</font>.</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="FONT-VARIANT: small-caps; DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt; FONT-WEIGHT: bold">Some academic studies indicate that political spending may not have a positive impact on the company&#8217;s bottom line and, in fact, may erode shareholder value</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 0pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 0pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">While there are academic studies on both sides of this issue, a recent literature review submitted as comment to the SEC in support of a petition that would require public companies to disclose corporate political spending, by Professor Michael Hadani of Long Island University, finds that political spending may not benefit, and in some cases, could harm a company&#8217;s bottom line.</font></div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block"><br>
</div>

<div style="TEXT-INDENT: 0pt; DISPLAY: block; MARGIN-LEFT: 36pt; MARGIN-RIGHT: 36pt" align="left"><font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-FAMILY: Times New Roman; FONT-SIZE: 10pt">&#8220;To summarize, this report described the findings of a large scale literature meta-analysis, a large scale empirical analysis and supplemental findings. <font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-WEIGHT: bold">All of which strongly indicate that firms' political spending, in particular contributions to policy makers, at best has an insubstantial impact on their bottom line and more often results in a negative effect of firm financial performance. </font>Shareholders, as noted above, are already concerned with the opaque nature of firms&#8217; political spending, and with good reason. The results of my empirical analysis alongside the analysis of others <font style="DISPLAY: inline; FONT-WEIGHT: bold">provide solid evidence as to the strong possibility that firms' political spending may harm their bottom line over time</font>.&#8221;<font style="FONT-SIZE: 70%; VERTICAL-ALIGN: text-top">8</font></font></div>
